# Threshold_73
## Artist's Manifesto

## Team

- `Salih Çimen` — `20230808075`
- `Arda Akıncı` — `20230808077`
- `Kerem Düz` — `20220808053`

There are songs that survive because they are large, and there are songs that survive because they are exact. Bob Dylan's `Knockin' on Heaven's Door` belongs to the second kind. It is not long. It is not ornate. It does not explain itself. It opens a small space and lets an entire human condition pass through it: the instant when a role can no longer be carried, when endurance changes shape, when the self that has been performing duty, fear, or identity reaches a limit and asks to be released.

`Threshold_73` began from that limit.

This project is my attempt to build a digital room inside the song without reducing the song to decoration. I did not want to make a "Bob Dylan themed interface," and I did not want to make a standard AI chatbot that happened to quote a few lyrics. The assignment made that temptation impossible to ignore, because the easiest version of this project would have been technically functional and spiritually empty. The brief asked for something much harder: not a product, not a report, but an artwork. It asked for technical ability, historical awareness, and philosophical honesty at the same time. That challenge is exactly what made the project worth making.

The title `Threshold_73` contains the whole argument of the work in compressed form. `73` anchors the project to a precise historical moment: the year of `Pat Garrett & Billy the Kid`, the year the song entered the world, the year anti-war refusal and draft exhaustion carried a particular moral weight in the United States. `Threshold` is the interpretive move. It is my way of saying that I do not hear "heaven's door" primarily as theology. I hear it as a boundary-state. A crossing. A release from an old form of selfhood. A moment in which something has to be laid down before anything else can begin.

That is why the medium had to be an interactive web experience.

If I had chosen a poster, a video essay, or even a linear short film, I could have interpreted the song, but I could not have asked the viewer to enter it. This project needed a form that could stage hesitation, approach, confession, waiting, reply, and closure. The web was not simply a convenient medium; it was the correct one because it could turn spectatorship into participation. A user does not only look at `Threshold_73`. They move through it. They scroll through a prologue that places them inside a historical and emotional atmosphere. They arrive at a room. They type something they are ready to leave behind. They wait while the chamber gathers itself. Then the work answers back. This procedural sequence matters. It transforms the project from a themed interface into a ritual structure.

I chose a threshold chamber, a radio language, and a staged voice system because the song itself is suspended between intimacy and performance. Dylan's Nobel lecture stayed with me while I was developing this project, especially the idea that songs are fulfilled not only on the page but in hearing, voicing, and living. That insight became architectural. I did not want the interaction to feel like submitting text to a machine and receiving output. I wanted it to feel overheard, transmitted, carried across a room. The language of radio helped me do that. Radio is haunted by distance. It is always a little unstable, a little historical, a little bodily. It suggests frequency, interference, tuning, arrival. It also carries the emotional texture of another era without becoming nostalgia for its own sake.

The most important artistic choice in the project is the decision to reinterpret the song through voices rather than through exposition. Instead of building one authoritative narrator, I created a council. `The Sheriff` speaks from weariness, role exhaustion, and the original cinematic DNA of the song: the dying lawman in `Pat Garrett & Billy the Kid`. `The Activist` speaks from the pressure of public history, from `1973` as a year of draft fatigue, refusal, and moral dissent. `The Balladeer` is not Dylan, and is not meant to imitate Dylan. Instead, that voice tries to do what songs often do at their best: gather contradiction, turn argument into cadence, and leave behind a more luminous line than analysis alone can produce. The final `Coda` then compresses the exchange into a single inscription. I wanted the structure to feel like moving through positions of consciousness rather than moving through features.

What caught me most deeply in the song was the line about the badge.

`Mama, take this badge off of me / I can't use it anymore.`

That line is astonishing because of how much it carries without theatrics. A badge is practical, visible, social. It is role made metal. It is duty you can pin to the body. In the context of the film, it belongs to a dying sheriff. In a wider human sense, it becomes any identity that once organized a life but no longer fits: competence, obedience, usefulness, professionalism, toughness, resilience, control. The line does not merely say "I am dying." It says: the thing that named me can no longer be worn. The role has reached its end before the person has finished feeling it.

That is the emotional center of `Threshold_73`.

I wanted the project to speak to contemporary versions of the badge: the student identity that survives by performance, the self structured by expectation, the habit that once functioned as protection, the posture that helped someone endure one chapter of life but becomes suffocating in the next. One reason the song remains powerful is that it does not force these meanings. It leaves space for them. The project tries to honor that openness by asking the user not "What do you think this song means?" but "What are you finally ready to leave behind?"

Historically, I did not want `1973` to be used as a mood board. That would have been superficial, and the assignment clearly rejects superficial reference. The period matters because it carries a specific atmosphere of endings: the Western myth fraying, public trust collapsing, the Vietnam era exhausting itself, refusal becoming morally charged rather than merely rebellious. In that light, the song's imagery becomes more than personal sadness. The badge is not only identity; it is institution, obligation, the fatigue of inherited roles. The buried guns are not only props; they suggest laid-down defense, relinquished violence, the abandonment of survival strategies that have become spiritually expensive.

At the same time, I was interested in the afterlife of the title beyond `1973`. The `1997` film `Knockin' on Heaven's Door` reframes the phrase as something closer to a last road, a final desire, an approach to the sea rather than a sermon. That alternative context helped me sharpen my interpretation. The project does not treat heaven's door as a destination map. It treats it as a state of transition in which the self becomes lighter by surrendering what can no longer be carried. That is why the work's atmosphere is not purely tragic. It is elegiac, but it is also releasing.

AI, in this project, was never one thing.

At different moments it behaved as a tool, a collaborator, a performer, a dramaturg, and a mirror. As a tool, it allowed me to design a system in which multiple textual perspectives could be orchestrated and voiced in sequence. As a collaborator, it produced language I then curated, constrained, rejected, normalized, or sharpened. As a performer, it literally became voice through TTS, embodying the room's answers. As a mirror, it revealed where my own interpretive choices were vague or dishonest: if the output drifted toward generic uplift, parody, or empty lyricism, that usually meant I had not yet defined the work's ethical center precisely enough.

This matters because I do not believe that using AI absolves the maker of authorship. The assignment makes that point very clearly, and I agree with it completely. AI can generate material. It cannot decide what the work is for. In `Threshold_73`, authorship lives in selection, refusal, shaping, pacing, architecture, and tone. It lives in deciding that the user should first be led through a scroll theater instead of a form. It lives in deciding that the last line should not be a motivational slogan but a ritual coda. It lives in choosing that the voices must be historically grounded but not reduced to impression or impersonation. AI gave me text and voice material; the work emerged from deciding what kind of room those materials belonged to.

Technically, the project uses AI not as isolated tricks but as linked dramatic functions. A language model, accessed through the Groq API, generates the staged responses. A structured orchestration layer controls how those responses relate to one another. A curated context selection system keeps the historical references active inside the reply logic. Piper transforms the responses into voiced presence. None of those parts, on its own, would have been enough. If there were only text, the room would lose embodiment. If there were only voice, it would lose interpretive complexity. If there were only one agent, the song's tension between resignation, refusal, and lyric release would flatten into a single perspective. If there were no curated historical context layer at all, the project might still feel atmospheric, but it would no longer feel answerable to `1973`.

That last point matters to me because I did not want to use history as ornament. I wanted it in the work's DNA. This is why the prompts and contextual fragments keep returning to the same loaded coordinates: the death scene, the badge, the guns, the draft, refusal, hearing as fulfillment, the threshold as release. The goal was not to produce a textbook account of the era. The goal was to ensure that every reply was faintly pressurized by that era, so that the room would never drift into generic self-help or purely contemporary therapeutic language.

The project also became personal in a way I did not anticipate at the start. The assignment asks, very directly, what "knocking on heaven's door" means in one's own life. My answer is that it names the moment when I recognize that continuity is not always fidelity. Sometimes what we call consistency is only fear wearing the mask of discipline. Sometimes a self survives by becoming legible to systems, expectations, grades, deadlines, or roles, and later discovers that the structure which once kept it functioning has begun to separate it from its own life. For me, the phrase "knocking on heaven's door" evokes exactly that threshold: the painful, necessary interval in which one asks whether endurance is still a virtue or whether it has become a way of refusing change.

That is why the work is not about death in a narrow sense. It is about forms of ending that allow life to continue differently. Graduation can be one of them. Leaving a city can be one of them. Breaking from a habit can be one of them. Stepping outside a role that once defined your worth can be one of them. Grief can be one of them too, because grief often asks us to carry a wound as identity long after its first necessity has passed. In all of these cases, the door is not something outside the self. It is the point at which the self stops insisting on the old costume.

I wanted the final coda screen to matter for that reason. It is not just a design flourish. It is the part of the project where the room briefly stops being interactive and becomes ceremonial. After the council of voices, the user is left with one short line at the center of the screen. That line is meant to feel like a decision has finally landed. Not a lesson. Not a summary. A landing. I wanted the user to feel, if only for a few seconds, that the room had recognized the burden they named and had given them permission to set it down without melodrama.

If the project succeeds, I hope it succeeds in exactly this way: not by proving that AI can write in voices or generate atmospheric interfaces, but by creating an experience in which technical systems, historical context, and emotional meaning become inseparable. I want the user to feel that the project understands something about farewell without aestheticizing pain into emptiness. I want the room to be beautiful, but not merely beautiful. I want it to be exact enough to linger.

The best part of the assignment, for me, is that it refuses to let technique be enough. It insists that we ask what a song does to us, what an era still asks of us, what our own thresholds are. `Threshold_73` is my answer to that invitation.

It says that the song's greatness lies in its simplicity, but its simplicity is not thinness. It is compression. It is a whole worldview carried in a few words: take this badge off of me. I can't use it anymore.

That line remains devastating because almost everyone has a badge they cannot use anymore, even if they have not yet admitted it. A project about `Knockin' on Heaven's Door`, then, should not only admire Dylan's line. It should build a place where someone can hear it happen inside themselves.

That is what I wanted this work to be.

Not an explanation of the door.

A room in which someone is allowed to knock.

## Team Contributions

Although each member had a primary area of ownership, `Threshold_73` was developed as a shared artwork rather than as three isolated tasks. Conceptual framing, iteration, and final decision-making were discussed collaboratively throughout the project.

- `Salih Çimen`
  Contributed primarily to the backend and AI pipeline: FastAPI integration, LangGraph orchestration, provider flow, staged response logic, and TTS system wiring. Also took part in system shaping, testing, and final integration decisions across the whole experience.

- `Arda Akıncı`
  Contributed primarily to the frontend visual design and interaction layer: layout, motion, atmosphere, staging, threshold chamber presentation, and the overall aesthetic coherence of the web experience. Also contributed to iterative UX refinement, demo polish, and parts of the LangGraph orchestration logic during the development process.

- `Kerem Düz`
  Contributed primarily to the manifesto writing and reflective framing of the work, helping articulate the philosophical, historical, and personal meaning of the project. Also contributed to backend development, project interpretation, content refinement, and the shaping of the experience beyond its technical core.

In practice, the work remained collaborative across all three areas. Backend decisions affected the tone of the writing, visual decisions affected the emotional meaning of the system, and manifesto work helped refine the project's technical and artistic direction. For that reason, the final result should be understood as a balanced team production with differentiated primary responsibilities rather than strictly separated labor.

## AI Tools And Models Used

The active submission version of `Threshold_73` uses the following AI-related tools and models:

- `Groq` API for LLM-based text generation
- `LangGraph` for multi-agent orchestration and response sequencing
- `Piper` for text-to-speech synthesis
- a curated historical context selection layer authored within the project to keep the song's cinematic and historical references active in the generation flow

These tools function inside a human-authored artistic system. The conceptual framing, role design, historical interpretation, interface decisions, audio staging, curation, and final meaning of the work remain the responsibility of the team.
